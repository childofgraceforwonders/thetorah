Theological Analysis

The Divinity of Jesus

1. Understanding God's Nature

Before examining the Son, it is crucial to understand the nature of God the Father. Theology asserts that God is an infinite entity who cannot be fully quantified or contained by human reason or language. He is spirit, and our finite, physical minds struggle to comprehend His infinite, non-physical reality. We do not get to dictate how God manifests; He is sovereign.

"For my thoughts are not your thoughts, neither are your ways my ways, declares the LORD. For as the heavens are higher than the earth, so are my ways higher than your ways and my thoughts than your thoughts." (Isaiah 55:8-9)
"But will God indeed dwell on the earth? Behold, heaven and the highest heaven cannot contain you..." (1 Kings 8:27)
"[He] who alone has immortality, who dwells in unapproachable light, whom no one has ever seen or can see." (1 Timothy 6:16)

Understanding this helps frame the doctrine of the Trinity. God is one, yet He has manifested Himself in different forms or persons without compromising His oneness. His Word is not merely a sound or a message; it is a perfect, divine expression of Himself—a person *within* His own being.

2. Jesus as the Word of God

This brings us to the foundational concept of Jesus as the "Word" (Greek: *Logos*). This title is the theological bridge to understanding His divine nature as a manifestation of the infinite God.

Core Passage:

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."
"And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us..." (John 1:1, 14)

Theological Breakdown:

  • The Word is Eternal: It existed "in the beginning."
  • The Word is Distinct from God (the Father): It was "with God."
  • The Word is God in Essence: It "was God."
  • The Word is Jesus: It "became flesh."

Therefore, the logic follows: Jesus is the Word, and the Word is God. He is the eternal self-expression of the uncontainable God made manifest in human form.

3. How Theologians Explain This

Major Christian thinkers have wrestled with and clarified this "paradox" of being both *with* God and *was* God.

Origen (c. 184–c. 253):

*One of the first to systematically analyze the Logos. He taught that the Word was eternally "begotten" (not created) of the Father. For Origen, the Word is divine by nature but distinct in person from the Father.*

Athanasius (c. 296–373):

*A champion of Jesus's full divinity. He famously argued, "God became man so that man might become God." For Athanasius, Jesus must be "true God from true God" because if the Word were a lesser, created being, He could not grant salvation or fully reveal the Father.*

Augustine (354–430):

*Developed the psychological analogy for the Trinity. He described the Father as the one who eternally speaks, the Son (Word) as the perfect thought or idea that is spoken, and the Holy Spirit as the love that binds them.*

4. The Personified Word in the Old Testament

The idea of God's Word as an active, personal agent was not new to the New Testament. This laid the groundwork for John's revelation of the Word becoming flesh.

"After this, the word of the LORD came to Abram in a vision... Then the word of the LORD came to him..." (Genesis 15:1, 4)
"And the LORD continued to appear at Shiloh, for the LORD revealed himself to Samuel at Shiloh by the word of the LORD." (1 Samuel 3:21)
"By the word of the LORD the heavens were made..." (Psalm 33:6)
"He sent out his word and healed them..." (Psalm 107:20)
"He sends out his command to the earth; his word runs swiftly." (Psalm 147:15)
"so shall my word be that goes out from my mouth; it shall not return to me empty, but it shall accomplish that which I purpose..." (Isaiah 55:11)

Jesus's own words carried profound claims of equality with God the Father.

6. Further Scriptural Affirmations

Beyond Jesus's own claims, the Apostles and Old Testament prophets explicitly attribute deity to him.

"The Son is the radiance of God’s glory and the exact representation of his being, sustaining all things by his powerful word." (Hebrews 1:3)
"‘I am the Alpha and the Omega,’ says the Lord God, ‘who is, and who was, and who is to come, the Almighty.’ ... 'I am the First and the Last.'" (Revelation 1:8, 17)
"For to us a child is born... and his name shall be called Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace." (Isaiah 9:6)
"He is the image of the invisible God... For by him all things were created... and in him all things hold together." (Colossians 1:15-17)
"For in him the whole fullness of deity dwells bodily." (Colossians 2:9)
"...waiting for... the appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior Jesus Christ." (Titus 2:13)
"...by the righteousness of our God and Savior Jesus Christ." (2 Peter 1:1)

7. Scriptural Objections & Refutations

Arguments against Jesus's divinity often use his own words. Here are the most common objections and their standard theological refutations.

Objection VerseThe Argument MadeTheological Refutation
Mark 10:18"Why do you call me good? No one is good—except God alone."Jesus isn't denying his divinity; he's challenging the man to consider the implication: "If you recognize me as good, you must recognize that I am God."
John 14:28"The Father is greater than I."This refers to Jesus's role during his earthly incarnation (*kenosis*), not his essential nature. In his humanity, he voluntarily submitted to the Father.
Matthew 24:36"...not even the Son, but the Father only."This points to the voluntary limitations Jesus accepted in his human nature. As God, He is omniscient; as man, He modeled submission to the Father.
John 20:17"I am ascending to... my God and your God."As a perfect human representative, Jesus relates to the Father as his God. This doesn't negate his divinity, which is affirmed when Thomas worships him as "My Lord and my God!" eleven verses later.

8. Deep Dive: Hebrews 1

Hebrews 1 is a powerful argument for Jesus's superiority over angels, culminating in a direct address where the Father calls the Son "God."

"But of the Son he says, 'Your throne, O God, is forever and ever... therefore God, your God, has anointed you...'" (Hebrews 1:8-9)

Linguistic and Contextual Analysis:

  1. The Quotation: The author is quoting Psalm 45:6-7, applying this exalted title directly to Jesus.
  2. The Greek Address: The phrase "Your throne, O God" is a direct address (*vocative* case), not a description.
  3. The Speaker: The most natural reading is that God the Father is the speaker, addressing the Son with the divine title.

9. Other Scriptures

While Hebrews 1:8 is the most explicit instance, the concept is reinforced by the Father attributing divine actions and titles to the Son elsewhere.

"And, 'You, Lord, laid the foundation of the earth in the beginning...'" (Hebrews 1:10-12) Here, the Father applies a passage about the eternal, unchanging Creator God (YHWH) directly to the Son.

10. Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: Is Jesus the same person as God the Father?

A: No, and this is where we must be careful with our limited human language. The Bible shows a distinction in relationship: the Father sends, and the Son is sent. However, this is not a distinction of separate beings like two humans. It is a distinction of roles or manifestations *within* the single, undivided essence of the one God. The Son is the perfect, visible manifestation of the invisible Father.

Q2: If Jesus is God, why did he say "the Father is greater than I"?

A: This statement reflects Jesus's role in his human manifestation. In becoming human, he voluntarily operated in a position of submission to the Father to fulfill his mission. It is a statement about his function and role on Earth, not a statement of his eternal, divine nature, which is one with the Father.

Q3: What does it mean that Jesus is the "Word of God"?

A: The "Word" (*Logos*) is the eternal, perfect self-expression of the unquantifiable God ("I AM"). It is God's mind, reason, and power manifested. The Bible reveals that this Word is not an impersonal force but a divine Person who has always existed with God and as God, and who became human as Jesus.

Q4: Did the idea of God's "Word" being a person start with Christianity?

A: No. As shown in Section 4, the Old Testament already described God's Word as an active, personal agent that "came" to prophets, created the universe, and accomplished God's will. This existing Jewish concept was the foundation for understanding Jesus as that Word made flesh.

Q5: Is there a clear verse where God the Father calls Jesus "God"?

A: Yes. Hebrews 1:8 is the clearest instance. The author states that the Father says *to the Son*, "Your throne, O God, is forever and ever." Here, the Father directly addresses His own manifestation, the Son, with the title "God."
